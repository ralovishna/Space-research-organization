Global Contributions of ISRO

Bridging Nations Through Space Technology and Collaboration

ISRO (Indian Space Research Organisation) has contributed significantly to both developing and developed nations, supporting various countries through technological collaborations, satellite launches, and knowledge-sharing initiatives.

Satellite Launch Services

For Developing Countries:
ISRO has launched satellites for several developing nations, significantly enhancing their access to space technology. For example, in 2016, ISRO successfully launched the NIGCOMSAT-1R satellite for Nigeria, which provides communication services, helping to improve telecommunication and broadcasting capabilities in the country. Additionally, in 2017, ISRO launched Brazilsat B4 for Brazil, aiding in telecommunications and contributing to the nation's technological development.


For Developed Countries:
ISRO's reputation as a reliable launch provider extends to advanced nations as well. In 2014, the PSLV launched the Spot 7 satellite for France, showcasing ISRO's capability to handle complex payloads for high-tech applications like Earth observation. Similarly, in 2018, ISRO launched GSAT-29 for the Indian Space Research Organisation, in partnership with Germany, demonstrating its collaborative efforts with developed nations to advance satellite technology and applications.

Disaster Management Support

For Developing Countries:
ISRO plays a crucial role in disaster management for developing nations by providing vital satellite data and remote sensing information. For instance, during the 2015 floods in Chennai, India, ISROâ€™s satellite imagery was used to assess the extent of flooding and facilitate timely responses. This support extends to countries like Nepal, where satellite data is utilized for monitoring landslides and floods, helping authorities improve disaster preparedness and recovery efforts.


For Developed Countries:
In collaboration with advanced nations, ISRO shares critical data with agencies such as NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA). Following events like Hurricane Katrina, ISRO's satellite imagery has been integrated into global disaster management frameworks to enhance situational awareness and response coordination. This partnership fosters a more comprehensive approach to tackling natural calamities, ensuring that resources are deployed efficiently and effectively.

SAARC Satellite (South Asia Satellite)

For Developing Countries:
In 2017, ISRO launched the GSAT-9, also known as the South Asia Satellite, to improve connections among SAARC countries. This satellite provides important telecommunications and weather data, helping developing countries in the region.
The South Asia Satellite shows ISRO's commitment to supporting neighboring countries with technology, helping them grow and become more resilient.

Key Benefits for Developing Countries:

Telemedicine: The satellite makes it easier for people in remote areas of countries like Nepal and Bhutan to access medical care and expert advice, improving health services.

Disaster Management: It offers real-time weather information, which helps countries like Bangladesh and Sri Lanka prepare for and respond to natural disasters, such as floods and cyclones.

Telecommunications: The satellite strengthens communication networks, allowing better connectivity and information sharing, which is essential for economic growth and cooperation.

Educational and Capacity-Building Programs

For Developing Countries:
ISRO actively supports developing nations through various training programs. For example, the Indian Technical and Economic Cooperation (ITEC) program offers training for scientists and engineers from countries like Ethiopia and Vietnam. Additionally, the UNNATI (UNispace Nanosatellite Assembly & Training by ISRO) initiative provides hands-on training for participants to learn how to build nanosatellites, helping them gain valuable skills and knowledge.


For Developed Countries:
ISRO also collaborates with advanced nations such as the United States and Germany to promote knowledge exchange. These partnerships focus on areas like remote sensing and space exploration. At the UN Centre for Space Science and Technology Education in Asia and the Pacific (CSSTEAP), ISRO conducts programs that facilitate international sharing of knowledge and expertise, helping to strengthen global cooperation in space science.

Joint Space Missions

ISRO has formed important partnerships with leading space agencies such as NASA, ESA, and ROSCOSMOS to conduct missions that enhance our understanding of space

One notable project is NISAR (NASA-ISRO Synthetic Aperture Radar), an upcoming joint Earth observation mission with NASA. This mission aims to monitor changes in Earth's land and ice, providing valuable data for climate research and environmental monitoring. By combining expertise and resources from both agencies, NISAR will contribute significantly to our understanding of global changes and support efforts in addressing climate-related challenges.

These joint missions reflect ISRO's commitment to international collaboration, fostering advancements in space science for the benefit of all nations.

Affordable Access to Space Technology

ISRO offers cost-effective launch services, making it easier for countries and organizations to access space technology without breaking the bank.

Benefits for Developing Countries:
One notable example is ISRO's launch of the DMC3 (Disaster Monitoring Constellation) satellites for the UK-based company Airbus Defence and Space in 2015. This collaboration allowed countries like Nigeria and Algeria to enhance their satellite capabilities at a fraction of the cost they would have incurred elsewhere. ISRO's competitive pricing enables developing nations to launch essential satellites, such as communication and weather monitoring systems, which are crucial for their development and disaster management efforts.


Benefits for Developed Countries:
In 2021, ISRO launched SEASAT-2, a satellite for the United States, using its PSLV (Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle). The launch demonstrated how ISRO offers reliable and affordable alternatives to established space launch providers like SpaceX and Arianespace. Agencies and commercial entities in developed countries increasingly choose ISRO for its budget-friendly options, which help them save costs while achieving their space goals.

Weather and Climate Monitoring

ISRO is instrumental in weather and climate monitoring through its Earth observation and weather satellites, especially the INSAT and GSAT series.

Support for Developing Countries:
ISRO's INSAT-3A satellite, launched in 2003, has been crucial for providing meteorological data to countries like Bangladesh and Sri Lanka. For instance, during the severe cyclones that have affected these nations, INSAT-3A's data has been used for accurate weather forecasting and timely warnings, significantly enhancing disaster preparedness and response efforts. This satellite system allows countries without their own advanced space assets to access critical weather information.


Enhancing Capabilities for Developed Countries:
ISRO also collaborates with developed nations to enhance climate monitoring. For example, the European Organisation for the Exploitation of Meteorological Satellites (EUMETSAT) utilizes data from ISRO's satellites to improve weather forecasting in Europe. In 2020, data from ISRO's GSAT-19 satellite was used in conjunction with European satellites to track and analyze weather patterns across the continent, thereby improving the accuracy of climate models.

International Space Station (ISS) Collaboration

ISRO has engaged with NASA and other space agencies regarding collaboration on the International Space Station (ISS), focusing on advanced research and technology development.

ISRO's contributions to the global space community are evident through its satellite technology and research. For instance, the Chandrayaan-2 mission included payloads designed for scientific collaboration that align with studies conducted on the ISS. The data and insights gathered from such missions enrich the broader scientific community's understanding of lunar geology and planetary science, which is relevant to ongoing research on the ISS.

Additionally, ISRO has been active in forums such as the International Space Exploration Coordination Group (ISECG), where it shares its expertise in satellite technology and Earth observation. This participation has helped shape international discussions about the use of space for monitoring climate change and disaster management, areas that are also important for ISS research.

These examples illustrate how ISRO is positioned to contribute to international space efforts, providing valuable insights and technology that support global research initiatives, including those conducted on the ISS.

Support in Navigation Systems

ISRO's NavIC (Navigation with Indian Constellation) is a regional satellite navigation system that provides accurate positioning services, complementing global systems like the U.S.-based GPS and Europe's Galileo.


Benefits for Neighboring Countries:
NavIC is especially valuable for countries in South Asia. For instance, in 2021, the Indian government made NavIC services available to neighboring countries like Nepal and Bhutan. This was highlighted during the launch of the NavIC-enabled smartphone applications, which support various local industries, including agriculture and transportation. In Nepal, farmers have started using these applications to optimize crop yields based on precise weather forecasts and soil conditions.


Regional Significance:
Moreover, during the COVID-19 pandemic, NavIC's precise positioning data was utilized by the Indian government to coordinate logistics for vaccine distribution which also benefited neighboring countries. In Bangladesh, the use of NavIC data has been integrated into navigation systems for improving transport and disaster management strategies, particularly during floods, which are a frequent occurrence in the region.

These examples illustrate how NavIC not only enhances navigation capabilities in India but also provides critical support to neighboring countries, promoting regional cooperation and development. ISRO's commitment to sharing NavIC services exemplifies its role in advancing technological collaboration in South Asia.
